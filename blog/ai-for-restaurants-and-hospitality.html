AI for Restaurants & Hospitality: Full-Service, Fast-Casual, Catering, Breweries

Brady Gay · May 19, 2026 · ~3 min read

Restaurants are the hardest business in America to operate at margin. The work is high-volume, low-ticket, hourly-staffed, and the owner is usually doing the books at 11pm after a 14-hour day. AI doesn't fix the food. It fixes everything around the food — the reservations going unanswered, the catering inquiries sitting in the GM's inbox, the vendor invoices stacking up, the inconsistent online ordering. Here's where it earns its keep across full-service, fast-casual, catering ops, and breweries.

The reservation + catering inquiry leak

Every restaurant has the same problem: the phone rings during a dinner rush, the host can't pick up, the call goes to voicemail. Reservations get missed. Worse — a catering inquiry for a 60-person event sits in voicemail until Tuesday afternoon, and by then they've called another spot.

The AI Receptionist answers every call. Books reservations against OpenTable / Resy / Tock per your rules (table mix, server availability, special-occasion notes). Captures catering inquiries with all the right detail — date, headcount, dietary restrictions, drop-off vs. on-site, budget — and routes them to the GM with everything pre-qualified. The host stops drowning. The GM stops fishing voicemail for leads.

Online ordering — the part that's actually losing you money

DoorDash, UberEats, Grubhub, Toast Online — every restaurant runs four ordering channels and none of them are integrated. The AI Office Manager reads inbound orders across all of them, surfaces conflicts (kitchen capacity vs. promised delivery time), flags wrong-address orders before they go to the driver, and runs the daily reconciliation against your POS so you actually know what each channel netted you after fees.

Catering ops: the highest-margin work, the worst process

Catering is the highest-margin work most restaurants do — and the worst-organized. Inquiry → menu proposal → contract → 50% deposit → final headcount → balance → execute → invoice. Every step is a missed handoff waiting to happen.

The AI Sales Navigator owns this. Builds the menu proposal against your catering deck. Drafts the contract. Sends the deposit invoice. Pulls the final-headcount confirmation 7 days out. Sends the balance invoice. Schedules the prep timeline back to your kitchen. The owner stops being the bottleneck on every catering deal. Most operators see catering revenue go up 30–40% in the first quarter just because nothing's getting dropped.

Vendor + supplier management

A 50-seat restaurant has 12–18 vendors: produce, protein, dry goods, dairy, paper, beer, liquor, wine, cleaning, linen, equipment, pest, hood, grease. Each one has invoices, statements, occasional disputes. The AI Bookkeeper reads vendor invoices, categorizes them into your COGS buckets, flags pricing drift ("this beef supplier is up 8% this month"), runs the AP cadence, and preps month-end close. The owner gets back the Sunday afternoons they currently lose to QuickBooks.

Reviews + reputation

Hospitality lives or dies on its Google + Yelp + TripAdvisor reputation. The AI Office Manager asks happy guests for reviews at the right moment (a personalized text 24 hours after a great visit), flags unhappy ones privately to you (before they post a one-star), and drafts owner-response replies to public reviews in your voice. Most operators don't realize how much margin a 0.4-star rating bump captures.

Where AI is hype for restaurants

The honest limits matter. AI does not cook. It does not run service. It does not catch a slow ticket on the line. It does not replace a great GM's instincts about table mix on a Saturday night. Anything that promises to "transform your restaurant with AI" is selling you a chatbot or a buzzword. The wins are unglamorous: reservations captured, catering deals closed, vendors managed, books reconciled, reviews handled. Quiet money. Compounds fast.
