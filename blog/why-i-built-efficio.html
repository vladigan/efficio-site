Why I built an AI team for myself first

Brady Gay · Head of Your AI Team · Miami
8 min read

There is a moment in every operator's life when the math of the day stops working. Mine was on a Wednesday. I was running four companies — government contracting at BNGCENT, automated trading at ApexTrader, a SaaS for job seekers at Career Copilots, and the firm doing all of it — and I was on hour eleven of a workday that had started in the dark. There was a lead in the inbox that needed a reply. There was a quote that needed to go out. There was a Sunday morning conversation that should have happened on Friday afternoon. There was a dashboard, somewhere, telling me everything I already knew. I closed the laptop. I made the bad decision to open it back up. I closed it again.

That was the moment I understood what I had been getting wrong for two years.

I had spent those years buying tools. Every operator I know has spent those years buying tools. CRMs, scheduling apps, voice agents, email warmers, lead scrapers, AI writers, calendar overlays, dashboards that promised to consolidate the other dashboards. The premise of the market was that if you stitched enough tools together you would, eventually, get to a place where the work ran itself. I had stitched. The work was not running itself. The work was waiting for me, the same way it had been waiting in 2022 and 2023 and 2024, with the only difference being that I now had a more expensive monthly software bill and a deeper sense that something had gone structurally wrong.

The thing that had gone structurally wrong is that AI tools are not an AI team.

A tool is a thing you operate. A team is a group of people — or specialists, or hires, the word matters less than the function — who own a job, learn how you specifically do it, and stay with you while you grow. A tool waits for you to push the button. A team takes the work off your plate before you knew it was on it. Every operator I have ever met has wanted a team. Every operator I have ever met has been sold a tool. The gap between those two things is the entire problem.

I stopped trying to stitch and started trying to build the team. Not metaphorically. Specifically. I sat down at the kitchen table with a notebook and asked, role by role, who I would hire if I had a real budget and a real HR department. I wrote down: a receptionist who would handle every call, text, and form. A dispatch admin who would route the work, send the "I'll be there at 11:42" SMS, keep the day from going sideways. A follow-up specialist who would touch every quote that hadn't closed, every customer at 30 and 60 and 90 days, every lapsed account I had forgotten about. An audit builder who would, when a prospect asked for the work-up, build it — pull their Google profile, identify their stack, write the leaks down, produce the PDF, brand it, queue the email. An onboarding concierge who would make sure that when a new client signed they did not, on day one, fall through the same operational seams that every new client had been falling through for years.

Five roles. Each one a real job. Each one a job I had been doing myself.

I did not need an app for any of them. I needed teammates.

So I built teammates. Specialists — that is the right word, because each one owns a function the way a human specialist would — that learn how I answer the phone, draft the quote, handle the rebook. Each one is tuned to my voice, my pricing, my pace. Each one queues its work for my approval. Nothing fires without my say-so. By month two, my AI Receptionist was sounding more like me than I sound at 7 in the evening. By month three, my AI Follow-Up Specialist had recovered more closed quotes than I had recovered in the prior twelve months combined. By month four, I stopped opening half the apps I had been paying for. By month six, I was running four companies as one person and the math of the day was, for the first time, working.

I also built the dashboard. One screen, every specialist, every conversation, every dollar surfaced. The kind of screen the operator running the team would want — not the IT team, not the consulting team, the operator. It is dark navy. It is dense where density earns trust and quiet where quiet earns confidence. It is the window into how the team is working. It is the only screen I keep open during the workday.

And — this is the part that matters most — I built the role of the head of the team. That role is me. I did not want to sell a tool that you log into and figure out alone. I wanted to deploy a team and stay with it. So that is what I do. When you sign with Efficio, you get the AI specialists, you get the dashboard, and you get me — week after week, tuning, hiring the next role, growing the department. Not a vendor checking in on a Slack channel once a quarter. A partner. The head of your AI team.

Every era of business operations has its labor model. The next one is hybrid: humans plus an AI team that learns alongside you. The question is not whether you adopt — it is who deploys yours.

The reason I am writing this essay is not to sell you something. It is to tell you what I learned. The last era belonged to operators who got good at buying tools. The next one will belong to operators who deploy teams. The compounding starts the day you decide which way you are going. Operators who deploy early will spend the rest of the decade compounding against operators who waited. Both groups will exist. Both groups will read essays like this. Only one group will get the next decade right.

I built Efficio because I needed it. I run it because I am proud of it. I am opening it up — three pilot slots this month, AI Specialist tier at $997 a month, first AI hire shipped in 14 days — because I genuinely want to know what an AI team looks like deployed inside operations other than mine. I want to learn the verticals I have not lived in. I want to meet the operators who have been doing this work the hard way for ten years and deserve a team that takes some of it off their plate.

This is what I built. It is what I run on. It is what I would have wanted to hire two years ago. The next era of business operations is here. The question is who deploys theirs first.
