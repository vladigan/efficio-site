Case Study 02 · Federal Contract Intelligence

$228K DIBBS Pipeline.
Scored Daily. No Manual Browsing.

A daily scraper-plus-scorer replaced the manual DIBBS portal grind — surfacing only the bids worth bidding, ranked by margin and supply-chain fit.

The Pain

The DLA DIBBS portal publishes hundreds of Request for Quotes daily across thousands of NSNs (National Stock Numbers). For a small federal contractor, the economics only work on a subset: parts you can actually source, at margins that clear overhead, before the bid window closes. The traditional approach is a human browsing the portal, running Google searches on each part, estimating supplier quotes from memory, and making gut-feel go/no-go decisions. That takes 2–3 hours a day, produces inconsistent decisions, and misses windows when the operator is busy. The $228K BID NOW queue shown here is what a systematic scoring engine surfaces from that noise — not the full DIBBS universe, just the qualified slice.

The Build

  • Daily DIBBS scraper: pulls active RFQs each morning (20–60 listings/day depending on cycle). Captures NSN, part description, quantity, delivery window, bidder count, and historical award price where available. Source: LLC/BNGCENT/ scraper module.
  • Claude-powered bid scorer (0–10): each RFQ is evaluated against five criteria — sourceable parts (known suppliers exist), competitive window (not already dominated by incumbent), delivery timeline feasibility, estimated margin given standard markup, and strategic FSC alignment with CAGE 12VS0's active cert categories. Only items scoring BID NOW surface to the action queue.
  • Priority OEM matching: a parallel process cross-references each NSN's manufacturer against a 20-OEM watchlist (Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers). Matches trigger a supplier outreach sub-workflow. Source: LLC/BNGCENT/docs/priority_oem_list_*.md.
  • Daily brief output: the full scored queue writes to a structured log. The cockpit surfaces the top BID NOW items with estimated contract value. Brady reviews, clicks approve on priority bids, moves on.

The Takeaway

The $228K figure isn't revenue — it's qualified opportunity surfaced from noise that used to require 2–3 hours of manual work to find. The system's value isn't the scraping (that's mechanical); it's the scoring rubric that filters 95% of listings out before they reach a human. That same logic applies to any business with a high-volume intake problem: supplier RFQs, inbound leads, job applications, customer support tickets. Build the scorer first. The execution layer follows.

Running a business where the real problem is finding signal in a noisy intake stream? That's exactly what this kind of build solves.
