Case Study 04 · Multi-Business AI Operations

17 Autonomous Pipelines.

Most AI agencies sell one service. This is what it looks like when the operator running those services actually eats his own cooking across four separate businesses — simultaneously.

The Pain

A solo operator running multiple businesses faces a specific kind of cognitive overhead: context-switching. The mental cost of moving from "what's my federal bid queue today" to "who replied to my cold email" to "did the job-search bot run" compounds throughout the day. The traditional answer is to hire — a VA for this, a scheduler for that, a specialist for the third thing. The AI answer is to build a single operations layer where every business reports up through the same interface, every agent runs in the background, and the operator's job is to review proposals and make calls — not to do the underlying work.

That's what the 17-pipeline stack shown here does. It isn't pitched as a concept. It runs every day.

The Takeaway

What makes this stack unusual isn't any individual pipeline — it's the shared control layer. Every agent from every business reports into the same approval inbox. Brady doesn't run four separate tool stacks; he reviews one queue. The cockpit pattern is what Efficio builds for clients: a custom version of this control layer, tuned to one business instead of four. The 17-pipeline count is unusual — most SMBs need 3–5 to see a step-change in output. The architecture scales down. The principle doesn't change.
