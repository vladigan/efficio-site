What Does an AI Receptionist Cost for a Small Business?

Brady Gay · July 2, 2026 · ~3 min read

If you've started looking into AI to cover your phones and front desk, the first question is usually the practical one: what's this going to cost me? Fair question. Here's an honest breakdown — what you're actually paying for, the pricing models you'll run into, and how to tell whether it's worth it for your business.

What you're actually paying for

"AI receptionist" covers a range of things, so the price depends on how much it does. At the low end, it's a simple after-hours text-back. At the high end, it's a full AI team that answers calls live, handles routine questions, books appointments into your calendar, and follows up on leads. More capability, more value — and more cost.

The main things that move the price:

  • Live call answering vs. text-only. Answering and holding a real phone conversation costs more than sending an automated text.
  • Booking and calendar integration. Turning a caller into a scheduled appointment is where a lot of the value is — and it takes real setup.
  • How many tools it connects to. Wiring into your CRM, calendar, and phone system takes work up front.
  • Volume. Some models charge per minute or per interaction, so a busy phone line costs more.

The pricing models you'll see

  • Per-minute. Common with answering services and some AI tools. Predictable when quiet, but a busy month can spike.
  • Flat monthly. A set fee for a set level of coverage. Easier to budget.
  • Setup fee plus monthly. You pay to build it, then a recurring fee to run it. Watch the upfront number.
  • Performance-based. You pay based on results — booked appointments or captured leads — rather than a fixed bill. Rare, but the most aligned with your interest, because the provider only wins when you do.

How to judge if it's worth it

Skip the sticker price for a second and think in terms of what one job is worth to you. If your average customer is worth a few hundred dollars — or a few thousand — then catching even one or two extra jobs a month that you'd otherwise have missed usually covers the entire cost, with room to spare.

So the real question isn't "how much does it cost?" It's "how many calls and leads am I losing right now, and what would catching them be worth?" Run that number first. For most small businesses, the missed opportunity is bigger than the fee.

Two things to watch for so you don't overpay:

  1. A big upfront setup cost before you've seen it work. You're taking all the risk before any results.
  2. Per-minute pricing on a high-volume line. Model your busiest month, not your quietest.
