AI Receptionist for Law Firms: Intake Without the Backlog

Brady Gay · July 7, 2026 · ~5 min read

For a small firm, the phone is intake, and intake is the practice. The problem is that the hours when potential clients call are the same hours you're in court, in consultations, or buried in a filing deadline. Someone with a legal problem rarely calls one firm — they work down the search results, and the firm that answers usually gets the consultation. Nothing personal; they needed a response and got one somewhere else.

An AI receptionist is how small firms stop losing intake to their own busyness. Here's what it does well, what must stay human, and what to ask before you trust one with your callers.

What an AI receptionist handles for a small firm

Every call answered, around the clock. No hold, no voicemail, no "our office is currently closed." The caller reaches a calm, competent voice at 8pm the same as at 10am — which matters, because legal problems don't respect office hours.

Your intake questions, asked consistently. What happened, when, in which county, who's on the other side. It gathers the names so your staff can run the conflict check — the AI collects; a human decides. Every intake arrives as a clean, complete summary instead of a two-line voicemail.

Screening for fit. Practice areas you don't take get a polite, immediate referral response you've written. Time-sensitive matters get flagged according to your rules. Your calendar stops filling with consultations you never should have taken.

Booking consultations. Qualified callers go straight onto your calendar — with a confirmation, what-to-bring instructions, and a reminder that quietly cuts your no-show rate.

Overflow and after-hours. If you have a receptionist or paralegal on the phones, keep them. The AI takes what they physically can't: the second simultaneous call, lunch hour, evenings, weekends.

What stays human — and should

  • Legal advice. Never from a machine, full stop. A well-built system is explicitly scripted to decline and route, not improvise.
  • Fee and retainer conversations. Quote ranges if you choose to, but the real conversation belongs to you.
  • Distressed callers. A family-law or criminal-defense call can be someone's worst day. The system should recognize distress and hand off warmly to your on-call process, not push through a script.
  • Conflict and acceptance decisions. The AI gathers the facts; a lawyer makes the call.

Confidentiality, plainly

What prospective clients tell you is sensitive even before an engagement letter exists — and some of it carries professional obligations. So put vendors through the same scrutiny you'd apply to any system that touches client information. Where are conversations stored? Who can access them? How long are they retained? Can you approve every script, so intake collects facts without inviting the caller's entire story?

A good intake script gathers what you need to schedule and screen — not a confession. Lean scripts protect callers and firms alike.

What actually changes day to day

Consultations appear on your calendar with a summary attached. The conflict-check names are already collected. Monday morning doesn't start with a voicemail backlog, and nobody plays three rounds of phone tag to schedule a thirty-minute meeting. You practice law during the hours you used to spend being your own receptionist.

Questions to ask any vendor

  • What will it refuse to answer, and how was that boundary built?
  • How does escalation to a human work — live, and after hours?
  • Do we write and approve every script and every fact it can state?
  • Where does the data live, and who at the vendor can see it?
  • Month-to-month, or a lock-in?
  • What does setup require from our staff, and who does the heavy lifting?
