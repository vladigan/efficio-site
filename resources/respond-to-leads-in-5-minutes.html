Speed to lead

How to Respond to Leads in Under 5 Minutes (Without Hiring Anyone)

Brady Gay · July 7, 2026 · ~5 min read

When someone fills out your form or calls your number, they're usually doing the same thing with two or three of your competitors. They have a problem, a free moment, and their phone in hand. Fifteen minutes later they're back in a meeting, on the road, or making dinner — and whoever reached them first has the inside track.

There's an old rule of thumb in sales: respond to a new lead within five minutes, because after that your odds start falling fast. Whether the true number is five minutes or fifteen, the direction isn't in question. Speed wins, and "we'll get back to you tomorrow" quietly loses.

Most owners already know this. Knowing isn't the bottleneck — capacity is. You can't answer a web form while your hands are inside a furnace or a patient's mouth. So here's the playbook, built for a business where nobody sits by the inbox.

Why speed beats polish

A fast, plain reply beats a perfect one an hour later. In the first few minutes, the lead is still thinking about their problem. They haven't compared prices, haven't heard back from anyone else, and haven't lost the urgency that made them reach out. An hour later, all of that has changed.

Speed also does your marketing for you. A business that responds in two minutes feels organized, awake, and easy to work with — before you've said a single word about your experience or your reviews.

The five-minute playbook

1. Acknowledge instantly, on the channel they used

A form fill should trigger a text within a minute or two. A missed call should trigger a text back. And the message should sound like a person, not a receipt. Not "Thank you for your inquiry. Someone will contact you shortly." Instead: "Hi Sarah — got your request about the water heater. Is it leaking now, or are you planning ahead?"

2. Ask one easy question

One question, answerable from a grocery-store line. It keeps the conversation alive and starts qualifying at the same time. Two questions feel like a form. Zero questions end the thread.

3. Offer times, not promises

The goal of the first five minutes isn't to close the sale — it's to get something on the calendar. "I can do Tuesday at 2 or Wednesday morning — either work?" beats "we'll be in touch" every single time. A booking link works too, as long as it's one tap away.

4. Call when a call is warranted

For urgent or big-ticket leads, a real phone call inside the window is worth the interruption. If they don't pick up, the text goes out immediately: "Just tried you — I'm around until 6 if you want to talk it through."

5. Don't stop at one attempt

Most leads don't answer the first touch. That's normal, not rejection. A simple cadence — same day, next day, day three, day seven — recovers a surprising share of the quiet ones. Every message stays short, and the sequence stops the moment they reply. Then speed applies all over again.

What this looks like when it runs

Say a lead comes in at 7:42 on a Tuesday night. At 7:43 they get a text with their name in it and one question. They answer at 7:51. Their question about service area gets answered, two times get offered, and by 7:58 there's an appointment on your calendar for Thursday — confirmation and reminder already scheduled. You find out about it over coffee the next morning.

That's the standard. Hold any system to it — human or software.

The honest problem with doing this manually

Running this playbook by hand means watching every channel, all day, including evenings and weekends — which is exactly when a lot of leads arrive. If you're on a roof, in a consult, or asleep, the window closes without you ever knowing it was open. Hiring helps, but one person covers forty hours of a 168-hour week, holds one conversation at a time, and has every right to stop checking the inbox at dinner.

This is a systems problem, and effort is the wrong tool for it.
