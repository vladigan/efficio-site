Admin time

How Much Time Do Small Business Owners Lose to Admin Work?

Brady Gay · July 7, 2026 · ~4 min read

Honest answer first: nobody can tell you exactly how much time you lose to admin. Surveys on this vary widely, and your business isn't a survey. What is consistent: almost every owner who actually measures it comes back with a bigger number than they guessed.

That's because admin doesn't take your time in blocks. It takes it in slivers.

Why the number always feels smaller than it is

Admin rarely appears on a calendar. It arrives between things — four minutes to answer "do you service my area?", six minutes to reschedule a Tuesday appointment, ten minutes of phone tag over a quote. No single sliver feels like work, so none of them get counted.

Two things make the real total worse than the sum of the slivers:

  • Switching cost. Every interruption charges you twice: the minutes it takes, plus the reload time to get your head back into whatever you were doing before the phone rang.
  • The evening shift. Admin that doesn't fit in the day migrates to 8pm — invoices, follow-ups, tomorrow's schedule — where nobody counts anything, because it's "just catching up."

Run a one-week admin audit

You don't need software for this. You need a week and a piece of paper.

1. Count events, not hours

Keep a tally, not a diary. Every time one of these happens, make a mark: a call answered, a missed call returned, a text or email about scheduling, a quote chased, a reminder sent, a no-show handled. Tally marks are enough — the goal is to see the frequency you've gone numb to.

2. Sort the marks into three buckets

  • Answering — the same questions, over and over: hours, pricing, availability, "did you get my message?"
  • Coordinating — booking, rescheduling, confirming, reminding.
  • Chasing — follow-ups you have to initiate: quotes that went quiet, unpaid invoices, review requests you keep meaning to send.

3. Do the math honestly

Assign real minutes to each mark — including the refocus time — and total the week. Then do the harder step: price those hours at what your hour actually sells for. An owner's hour is worth the billable work, the sales conversation, or the rest it displaces. Costing it at what an assistant would charge is how the leak stays invisible.

The second cost nobody audits

While you're doing admin, admin is doing damage in the other direction. The new lead who called at 2pm — while you were untangling a double-booking — didn't wait for you to finish. Admin doesn't just consume your hours; it makes you slow, and slow loses jobs to whoever answered first. The audit counts the hours. The leads that leaked out during those hours are the part no tally sheet catches.

What to hand off first

Not everything on your sheet should be automated. Keep the judgment work: pricing exceptions, an unhappy customer, anything where the honest answer is "it depends." Hand off the work that's repeatable, rule-based, and interruption-driven:

  • Answering the common questions on calls and texts
  • Booking, rescheduling, confirmations, and reminders
  • First response to every new lead, and the follow-up sequence after it
  • The polite nudges — quotes, invoices, reviews

A useful test: if you could write instructions a competent temp could follow, it can run without you.
